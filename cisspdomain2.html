Domain 2:   Asset Security.


  1. Information goes through a life cycle that starts with its acquisition and ends with its disposal.

  2. Each phase of the information life cycle requires different considerations when assessing risks and selecting controls.

  3. New information is prepared for use by adding metadata, including classification labels.

  4. Ensuring the consistency of data must be a deliberate process in organizations that use data replication.

  5. Data aggregation may lead to an increase in classification levels.

  6. Cryptography can be an effective control at all phases of the information life cycle.

  7. The data retention policy drives the timeframe at which information transitions from the archival phase to the disposal phase of its life cycle.

  8. Information classification corresponds to the information’s value to the organization.

  9. Each classification should have separate handling requirements and procedures pertaining to how that data is accessed, used, and destroyed.

  10. Senior executives are ultimately responsible to the shareholders for the successes and failures of their corporations, including security issues.

  11. The data owner is the manager in charge of a specific business unit and is ultimately responsible for the protection and use of a specific subset of information.

  12. Data owners specify the classification of data, and data custodians implement and maintain controls to enforce the set classification levels.

  13. The data retention policy must consider legal, regulatory, and operational requirements.

  14. The data retention policy should address what data is to be retained, where, how, and for how long.

  15. Electronic discovery (e-discovery) is the process of producing for a court or external attorney all electronically stored information (ESI) pertinent to a legal proceeding.

  16. Normal deletion of a file does not permanently remove it from media.

  17. NIST SP 800-88, Revision 1, "Guidelines for Media Sanitization", describes the best practices for combating data remanence.

  18. Overwriting data entails replacing the 1's and 0's that represent it on storage media with random or fixed patterns of 1’s and 0’s to render the original data unrecoverable.

  19. Degaussing is the process of removing or reducing the magnetic field patterns on conventional disk drives or tapes.

  20. Privacy pertains to personal information, both from your employees and your customers.

  21. Generally speaking, organizations should collect the least amount of private personal data required for the performance of their business functions.

  22. Data at rest refers to data that resides in external or auxiliary storage devices, such as hard drives or optical discs.

  23. Every major operating system supports whole-disk encryption, which is a good way to protect data at rest.

  24. Data in motion is data that is moving between computing nodes over a data network such as the Internet.

  25. TLS, IPSec, and VPNs are typical ways to use cryptography to protect data in motion.

  26. Data in use is the term for data residing in primary storage devices, such as volatile memory (RAM), memory caches, or CPU registers.

  27. Scoping is taking a broader standard and trimming out the irrelevant or otherwise unwanted parts.

  28. Tailoring is when you make changes to specific provisions in a standard so they better address your requirements.

  29. A data leak means that the confidentiality of the data has been compromised.

  30. Data leak prevention (DLP) comprises the actions that organizations take to prevent unauthorized external parties from gaining access to sensitive data.

  31. Network DLP (NDLP) applies data protection policies to data in motion.

  32. Endpoint DLP (EDLP) applies data protection policies to data at rest and data in use.

  33. Mobile devices are easily lost or stolen and should proactively be configured to mitigate the risks of data loss or leakage.

  34. Paper products oftentimes contain information that deserves controls commensurate to the sensitivity and criticality of that information.
